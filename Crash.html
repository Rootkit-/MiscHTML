The Codependency of Shadow and Traditional Banking

The codependency between shadow banks, real banks, and the Treasury market has created a massive structural risk. The situation as of March 2026 is that the global financial system is relying on extreme leverage to maintain stability. Traditional banks and shadow banks are now effectively joined at the hip. Major banks have over 2.5 trillion dollars in exposure to non bank financial institutions, including special purpose vehicles and private equity funds. This means traditional banks are the primary lenders to the shadow banks. If a shadow bank fails because its AI or real estate bets go bad, the loss hits the traditional bank balance sheet immediately.

Exorbitant Privilege and the Petrodollar Anchor

The connection to the U.S. Treasury market is even more dangerous. This is driven by the exorbitant privilege of the U.S. dollar, a term describing the unique advantage the United States has as the provider of the world’s reserve currency. Because the dollar is the global gold standard for trade, every other country is essentially forced to hold dollars and buy U.S. Treasuries to participate in the global economy. This creates a constant, artificial demand that allows the U.S. to borrow money at much lower interest rates than any other nation. This privilege is anchored by the petrodollar system, a 1970s agreement where oil producing nations like Saudi Arabia agreed to sell their oil only in dollars. In exchange for military protection, they recycle those oil profits back into U.S. Treasuries.

The Hidden Leverage in Offshore Holdings

However, this anchor is dragging. Recent Federal Reserve research from late 2025 and early 2026 revealed that Cayman domiciled hedge funds were underreporting their Treasury holdings by nearly 1.4 trillion dollars. When you add their actual holdings to the official data, the total long Treasury exposure for these private funds hit roughly 2.4 trillion dollars. These funds are not just buying Treasuries; they are borrowing against them to buy more. For every dollar of real investor money, they are borrowing 10 to 15 dollars. This is why the gross risk is so much higher than the actual size of the funds.

The Explosion of Basis and Swap Trades

While the basis trade, which involves betting on price gaps between Treasury bonds and futures, has hovered around 1.1 trillion dollars, a new strategy called the Swap Trade doubled in 2025. This leveraging of Treasuries against interest rate swaps added another 630 billion dollars in leveraged risk to the pile. This is financed through the repo market, where funds get overnight loans by pledging their Treasuries as collateral. Over 70 percent of these trades currently have zero or negative haircuts, meaning the lenders are not requiring any extra cash buffer to protect against a drop in value. The top 50 hedge funds control 90 percent of this activity, meaning the entire Treasury market now depends on a handful of firms.

The Rise of the Petroyuan and Systemic Fragility

This is a hair trigger for a systemic crash. As China and Saudi Arabia roll out the petroyuan and settle oil trades in Chinese currency, the global need to hold U.S. dollars and Treasuries is dropping. China has already slashed its Treasury holdings to roughly 680 billion dollars. If oil prices or other shocks cause a sudden spike in interest rates, the value of those Treasuries drops and triggers immediate margin calls. Because the leverage is so high, these funds have to sell their Treasuries instantly to get cash, driving prices lower and yields higher. In this system, Treasuries are no longer a safe haven; they are a source of contagion.

Fraud and the Foundation of Air

To understand how reckless this leverage has become, look at the recent collapse involving a BlackRock linked entity. A telecom company, Carriox, filed for bankruptcy after it was discovered they had over 500 million dollars in debt backed by less than 100,000 dollars in actual assets. The lenders, including BlackRock’s HPS unit, were essentially lending against an elaborate balance sheet of fake invoices and forged emails. This is the definition of shadow banking risk: massive piles of debt built on a foundation of air.

The Desperate AI Hail Mary

The shadow banks were not just being innovative. They were desperate. For years, these private lenders were stuck with a mountain of zombie companies which were walking dead firms kept alive only by low interest rates and accounting tricks. When the AI boom started, these funds viewed it as a perfect get out of jail free card. They poured everything into AI as a Hail Mary pass, hoping a massive 100x return would finally recoup the losses from their previous mistakes.

Energy Costs and the Profitability Wall

But that gamble is now hitting a wall. With oil prices surging toward 120 dollars a barrel and electricity costs through the roof, the AI payout is taking too long and costing too much. Data centers are energy gluttons, and those rising power bills are eating the profit margins that were supposed to pay back the lenders. Instead of AI saving the funds, the high operational costs are dragging them down even faster.

Illiquidity and the Gating of Funds

The real structural issue is that these shadow banks have their money tied up in a web of other risky assets like struggling commercial real estate. Office delinquency rates hit a record 12.34 percent in early 2026. Because energy costs are spooking the entire market, investors are panicking and trying to pull their cash out. This has forced giants like BlackRock to gate their funds, literally locking the doors and stopping withdrawals to prevent a total collapse. This creates a total liquidity dead end. Even if an AI company has a great product, the private banks they rely on for monthly cash are currently frozen because their other bets went sideways.

Payment-in-Kind and the Hidden Rot

To hide the rot, lenders are increasingly using payment in kind interest, which allows companies to just add their interest payments to the total loan balance instead of paying cash. PIK interest now accounts for nearly 9 percent of total investment income for major private credit funds. To make matters worse, these companies are trapped in the private market. They cannot just go public to get fresh cash because the big firms like BlackRock and Vanguard also run the massive index funds. These indexes are already dangerously top heavy with tech. Adding more money losing AI companies to the index would force these funds to sell off their other holdings, potentially triggering a market wide death spiral.

Fake Growth and the Survival of the Bottom 80 Percent

The picture is even bleaker when you look at what supported our growth last year. Half of the GDP gain in 2025 came from just healthcare and AI software. Healthcare is essentially an administrative overhead on the economy, not productive growth. While these sectors padded the numbers, the information and professional services sectors actually shed 173,000 jobs. At the same time, the bottom 80 percent of the country is in survival mode. Americans are now carrying a record 1.28 trillion dollars in credit card debt. Nearly one in four households is living paycheck to paycheck, spending over 95 percent of their income on basic necessities like housing and gas.

Tariffs and the Final Roadblock

Adding fuel to this fire are the 2025 and 2026 tariffs. A 25 percent tariff on high performance semiconductors is increasing the cost of the hardware needed for the AI buildout. This creates a roadblock for the very infrastructure that was supposed to be our saving grace. We have a system where the growth is fake, the debt is real, the costs are rising, and the exit doors are locked.
